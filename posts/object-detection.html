Robust Pedestrian Detection using Transformers

While Transformer-based models have revolutionized NLP and are now making heavy inroads into Computer Vision, they initially struggled to surpass the performance of CNN-based models in specific tasks like pedestrian detection. In our research paper presented at WACV 2024, we explored why this happens and how to fix it.

The Problem: One-to-One Matching

Most transformer-based detectors (like DETR) use a one-to-one bipartite matching during training. This means each ground truth object is matched to exactly one predicted box. While elegant, this often leads to sparse training signals and slower convergence, especially for smaller, crowded objects like pedestrians.

Our Solution: Many-to-One Matching

We proposed a Min-cost-flow based matching algorithm that allows for many-to-one matching. By allowing multiple candidate predictions to match with a single ground truth object during the middle stages of training, we provide a much richer gradient signal to the model.

This approach significantly improved miss rates across multiple challenging datasets (CityPersons, CrowdHuman) without adding any inference-time overhead.

Impact and Implementation

The resulting model is not only more accurate but also more robust to occlusions—a common problem in urban pedestrian detection. We've open-sourced our code and matcher to help the community experiment with these techniques.
